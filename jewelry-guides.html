Start with the Basics

Jewelry can feel confusing when every listing uses different words for metal, stone setting, finish, and care. This guide explains common terms in plain language so you know what to notice before choosing a piece.

Common Jewelry Metals

Gold

Gold is a classic jewelry metal known for its warm color and timeless look. You may see yellow gold, white gold, or rose gold. Gold purity is often described by karat, such as 10K, 14K, 18K, or 24K. Higher karat means more gold content, while lower karat pieces are mixed with other metals for added strength.

White Gold

White gold has a bright, silvery look, but it is usually made from yellow gold mixed with other metals or finished with rhodium plating. Over time, white gold may need re-plating to keep its bright white finish.

Rose Gold

Rose gold has a soft pink tone created by mixing gold with copper. The exact color can vary depending on the metal blend. It is often used for romantic, warm, and vintage-inspired jewelry styles.

Sterling Silver

Sterling silver is a popular silver jewelry metal with a bright, cool tone. It can tarnish over time, so it often needs gentle cleaning and careful storage. Look for retailer details about whether a piece is solid sterling silver, silver plated, or another silver-tone material.

Platinum

Platinum is a strong, naturally white precious metal often used in fine jewelry. It is known for its weight, durability, and resistance to tarnish. Because it is dense and rare, platinum jewelry may feel heavier and may cost more than similar styles in other metals.

Gold Plated, Gold Filled, and Vermeil

These are not the same as solid gold. Gold-plated jewelry has a thin layer of gold over another metal. Gold-filled jewelry has a thicker bonded layer of gold. Vermeil usually means gold layered over sterling silver. Always review the retailer page for the exact metal description.

Common Jewelry Settings

A jewelry setting is the way a stone or gem is held in place. The setting can affect the look, height, security, and care needs of a piece.

Prong Setting

A prong setting uses small metal claws to hold a stone. This style can let more light reach the stone, but the prongs should be checked over time because they can catch or wear down.

Bezel Setting

A bezel setting wraps metal around the edge of a stone. It often gives a smooth, modern look and may feel more protected than high prong styles.

Pavé Setting

A pavé setting places many small stones close together for a sparkling surface. It can look beautiful, but small stones and tiny metal beads may need gentle care.

Channel Setting

A channel setting holds stones between two strips of metal. This can create a smooth row of stones, often seen in bands and bracelets.

Halo Setting

A halo setting surrounds a center stone with smaller stones. It can make the center design look larger and more decorative.

Flush Setting

A flush setting places the stone low into the metal so the surface feels smoother. This can be useful for simple, low-profile jewelry styles.

Details to Notice

Jewelry Finishes and Details to Notice

The finish changes how a piece catches light. High polish looks shiny and reflective. Satin or brushed finishes look softer and more muted. Textured finishes can add dimension. Mixed-metal designs combine different metal tones, such as yellow gold with white gold or silver.

Jewelry Care

How to Care for Jewelry

  • Store pieces separately to help prevent scratches and tangling.
  • Keep jewelry away from harsh cleaners, chlorine, perfume, lotion, and hairspray when possible.
  • Use warm water, mild soap, and a soft brush for many simple pieces, but be careful with pearls, opals, turquoise, treated gems, and delicate stones.
  • Avoid ultrasonic cleaners unless the retailer or a professional jeweler says the piece is safe for that type of cleaning.
  • Review care instructions from the retailer because different metals, stones, and finishes may need different care.
