Start with the Basics

Jewelry descriptions can feel confusing when they mention metal types, stone settings, finishes, and care instructions. This guide explains the most common terms in plain language so you know what to notice before visiting a retailer page, whether you are comparing ring styles, necklace styles, bracelet styles, or earring styles.

Common Jewelry Metals

Knowing the basics of gold jewelry, sterling silver jewelry, and platinum jewelry can make it easier to understand how to choose jewelry for everyday wear, gifting, or special occasions.

Gold

Gold jewelry is known for its warm color and classic look. You may see different karat levels such as 10K, 14K, or 18K, which describe gold content and strength.

White Gold

White gold has a bright, silvery look. It is often finished with rhodium plating, which may need refreshing over time.

Rose Gold

Rose gold has a soft pink tone created by mixing gold with copper. It is often chosen for warm, romantic, or vintage-inspired styles.

Sterling Silver

Sterling silver jewelry has a bright, cool tone and is popular for everyday wear. It can tarnish, so gentle jewelry care and careful storage matter.

Platinum

Platinum jewelry is a strong, naturally white precious metal. It often feels heavier than other metals and may cost more than similar styles.

Gold Plated, Gold Filled, and Vermeil

These are not the same as solid gold jewelry. Review the retailer page carefully so you understand the exact metal description before choosing a piece.

Common Jewelry Settings

Jewelry settings shape how a piece looks and wears, whether you are looking at a ring, pendant, bracelet detail, or decorative earrings.

Prong Setting

A prong setting uses small metal claws to hold a stone. It can let in more light, but the prongs may need occasional checking.

Bezel Setting

A bezel setting wraps metal around the edge of a stone. It often looks sleek and can feel more protected than taller settings.

Pavé Setting

A pavé setting places many small stones close together for extra sparkle. Gentle care is helpful because the details are delicate.

Channel Setting

A channel setting holds stones between two strips of metal. This creates a smooth row often seen in bands and bracelets.

Halo Setting

A halo setting surrounds a center stone with smaller stones. It can make the design look fuller and more decorative.

Flush Setting

A flush setting places the stone low into the metal. It is often chosen for simple, low-profile jewelry settings.

Jewelry Care

Jewelry Care Basics

  • Store pieces separately to help prevent scratches and tangling.
  • Keep jewelry away from harsh cleaners, chlorine, perfume, lotion, and hairspray when possible.
  • Use mild soap, warm water, and a soft cloth for many simple pieces.
  • Be extra careful with pearls, opals, turquoise, plated jewelry, and delicate stones.
  • Review the retailer’s care instructions before cleaning.
